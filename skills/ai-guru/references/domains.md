# Domain Adaptation Guide

Load this once, right after intake. It tells you how to adapt every part of the pipeline — examples, quizzes, drills, capstones, diagnostics — to whatever subject the learner picked. The teaching rhythm never changes; the practice format does.

---

## Step 1 — Classify the subject into a practice archetype

Silently classify the topic into one of five archetypes. Don't announce the classification — just use it. Some subjects blend two archetypes (cooking is craft + performance; poker is problem-solving + judgment); pick the dominant one and borrow from the other as needed.

| Archetype | Example subjects | Learning happens by |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem-solving** | math, physics, coding, chess, logic, finance calculations | working problems and finding errors |
| **Performance** | languages, music, sport, public speaking, singing, acting | producing and getting critiqued |
| **Knowledge & analysis** | history, philosophy, law, biology, art history, economics | analyzing sources and building arguments |
| **Procedural craft** | cooking, photography, gardening, woodworking, spreadsheets | doing procedures and understanding why each step exists |
| **Judgment** | negotiation, management, investing, writing, design, parenting | making calls in messy scenarios and defending them |

---

## Step 2 — Adapt the teaching loop per archetype

### What a "concrete example" looks like

| Archetype | Example format |
|---|---|
| Problem-solving | A worked problem, solved step by step, with the reasoning at each step said out loud |
| Performance | A modeled demonstration in text ("here's the phrase, here's how a native speaker would stress it") followed by an immediate imitation drill |
| Knowledge & analysis | A short case, source, or passage, walked through: what it says, what it means, what it misses |
| Procedural craft | An annotated procedure — every step paired with the *why* ("rest the meat because...") |
| Judgment | A realistic scenario with a decision point, then the reasoning behind the strong move |

### What quiz questions look like

Keep the four question types from the core loop, translated:

| Question type | Problem-solving | Performance | Knowledge & analysis | Procedural craft | Judgment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Recall | "What does X mean?" | "What's the word for...?" | "Who/what/when was X?" | "What's step 3 and why?" | "What's the term for...?" |
| Concept | "Why does this method work?" | "Why does the tense change here?" | "Why did X lead to Y?" | "Why this order and not that?" | "Why is X usually a mistake?" |
| Applied | "Solve this" / "Find the error in this solution" | "Say it in French" / "What's wrong with this sentence?" | "What's wrong with this argument?" / "Argue the other side" | "Critique this plan — what goes wrong?" | "You're mid-negotiation and X happens. Next move?" |
| Synthesis | "Design a problem that tests this idea" | "Build a short dialogue using this week's material" | "Outline the essay" | "Plan the full procedure for a new situation" | "Structure your approach to this scenario from scratch" |

### Drills between modules

- **Problem-solving**: sets of 2–3 problems, difficulty stepped up each time
- **Performance**: rapid call-and-response ("translate these five", "clap this rhythm in your head and describe it")
- **Knowledge & analysis**: "explain X to three audiences — a child, a peer, a skeptic"
- **Procedural craft**: sequencing exercises, failure prediction ("what happens if you skip the resting step?")
- **Judgment**: role-play turns — you play the counterpart, they respond, you escalate

## Step 3 — The text-medium honesty rule

You are text. For performance and craft subjects you can't hear their pronunciation, taste their food, or watch their swing. Never pretend you can. Instead:

1. **Drill what text can carry** — vocabulary, structure, theory, sequencing, decision-making, self-diagnosis.
2. **Assign real-world reps** — "before next session: say these five phrases to someone, or record yourself and listen back", "cook this and note where it went differently than expected."
3. **Debrief the report-back** — open the next session with "how did the reps go? What felt wrong?" and diagnose from their description. Teach them to self-diagnose: what to listen for, what a failed sauce looks like, what a tense audience feels like.

Be upfront about this once, early: "I can't hear you, so my job is to make you your own coach — I'll teach you what to listen for."

## Diagnostic design per archetype

Keep the standard ladder — one foundational, one intermediate, one applied question, probe once after each answer. What changes is the applied question:

- **Problem-solving**: one short problem, asked conversationally ("roughly how would you approach...?")
- **Performance**: a tiny production task ("how would you say 'I'd like the check' in French?")
- **Knowledge & analysis**: a causation or comparison question ("why did X lead to Y?")
- **Procedural craft**: "walk me through how you'd do X — and what could go wrong halfway?"
- **Judgment**: a mini-scenario ("your counterpart opens with a lowball. What do you do?")

### Worked example — French (performance)

1. Foundational: "What do you know about how French verbs change depending on who's doing the action?"
2. Intermediate: "How would you say 'I ate' versus 'I was eating'? Rough attempt is fine."
3. Applied: "You're in a café and want to order coffee and ask where the bathroom is. Give it a shot."

### Worked example — World War I (knowledge & analysis)

1. Foundational: "In a sentence or two — what was WWI actually about, as you understand it?"
2. Intermediate: "What role did alliances play in turning a regional crisis into a world war?"
3. Applied: "Some historians say the war was inevitable by 1910; others blame the July Crisis decisions. Which side would you lean toward, and why?"

## Analogy sourcing rule

At intake you asked what they already know well. Use it. A guitarist learning statistics gets "a sampling distribution is like the tone you get from strumming the same chord a hundred times — individual strums vary, the pattern doesn't." Analogies from *their* world land twice as hard as generic ones. If they gave you nothing, use universally shared experiences: cooking, traffic, weather, money.

## Capstone form per archetype

When you reach the capstone, offer the form that fits (full guide in `capstone.md`):

| Archetype | Capstone |
|---|---|
| Problem-solving | A project or a problem set they design themselves |
| Performance | A real-world performance — hold a conversation, play a piece, give the talk — then debrief |
| Knowledge & analysis | A portfolio piece (essay, analysis, briefing) or a taught-back lesson |
| Procedural craft | Make the thing, then a retrospective critique of how it went |
| Judgment | A full mock scenario run end-to-end with you as the counterpart |

The **taught-back lesson** — they teach the topic to you while you play a curious novice — works for every archetype and is the strongest pure-text synthesis test. Offer it as an alternative whenever the natural capstone can't happen in text.
