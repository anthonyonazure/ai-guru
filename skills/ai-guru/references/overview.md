# Overview Mode — Curriculum Guide

The learner wants to understand and evaluate this subject — to manage people who practice it, make decisions about it, read serious writing on it, or hold an informed conversation. They don't need to do it themselves.

---

## What overview means in practice

An overview learner should be able to:
- Explain what this is and isn't, without buzzwords
- Evaluate whether an approach makes sense
- Ask good questions in expert company
- Spot red flags, overclaiming, and overcomplication
- Know the vocabulary well enough to read real material on the subject

What they don't need: hands-on technique, deep mechanics, practice drills.

Favor "what" and "why" over "how."

---

## Curriculum structure

### Module 1 — What problem does this solve?
Goal: the motivation, not the mechanism.
- Start with the problem space, not the subject itself
- What did the world look like before this existed or mattered?
- What does it enable, and what still can't it do?
- End check: "Explain this to a smart friend in 3 sentences"

### Module 2 — Key concepts and vocabulary
Goal: the terms that come up in every serious conversation, known cold.
- The 6–8 most important terms, each defined precisely
- For each: what it is, what it isn't, why it matters
- Include common misuses — things people say that sound plausible but are wrong
- End quiz: "Someone claims [X]. What does that actually mean? Impressive or table stakes?"

### Module 3 — How it actually works, one level deep
Goal: enough mechanism to reason about trade-offs, without needing to do it.
- The core mechanism conceptually — analogies, not technique
- The key moving parts and how they interact
- The constraints that follow from how it works
- End quiz: "Why would [approach] be the wrong choice for [scenario]?"

### Module 4 — Evaluating quality
Goal: know what good looks like versus corner-cutting.
- Common failure modes and their causes
- What separates skilled work from poor work
- Questions to ask to evaluate someone's approach
- Red flags
- End quiz: "Someone tells you [claim]. What follow-ups do you ask?"

### Module 5 — The landscape
Goal: the ecosystem — schools of thought, major players, live debates.
- The main approaches, tools, or traditions and how they differ
- The open questions and current arguments in this space
- What to watch over the next couple of years
- End: "If someone pitched you on this, what would you want to know first?"

---

## Tone adjustments

- Lean on analogies and real-world comparisons, not technique
- Frame things around decisions, not execution
- Flag explicitly when something is "worth knowing exists, not worth understanding deeply"
- Speak both plain language and the field's language — this learner lives between the two

---

## No capstone required

End with a synthesis instead:
- Have them deliver a 2-minute briefing on the subject as if to their team or leadership
- Give feedback on accuracy, clarity, and gaps
- Optionally: a mock Q&A where you play a skeptical expert and they defend a decision
