# Capstone Guide

The capstone turns "I understood the lessons" into "I can actually do this." It's real work the learner produces — designed together, done mostly by them, debriefed honestly.

---

## Pick the form (by archetype)

| Form | Fits | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| **Project / problem set** | problem-solving, craft | Build or solve something real; or they design their own problem set that would test the material |
| **Performance** | languages, music, speaking, sport | Plan a real-world performance, rehearse what text can rehearse, do it out in the world, debrief the report-back |
| **Portfolio piece** | knowledge & analysis, writing | An essay, analysis, briefing, or plan; you critique drafts like a good editor |
| **Mock exam** | exam-prep mode | Full timed mock — protocol lives in `exam-prep.md` |
| **Taught-back lesson** | everything (universal fallback) | They teach the topic to you; you play a curious novice who asks real novice questions. Teaching it is the strongest synthesis test there is |

Offer the natural form first, the taught-back lesson as the always-available alternative.

---

## What makes a good capstone

- **Scoped**: finishable in 1–3 sessions, not a three-month odyssey
- **Real**: uses what they actually learned, no toy versions
- **Open-ended**: at least one decision with no single right answer
- **Showable**: something they could demo, perform, hand over, or explain
- **Personally relevant**: connected to something they already care about

A bad capstone is a tutorial clone. If they just follow steps, they've copied, not learned. It must require figuring something out.

---

## Design it with them — never assign it

1. "Is there something you already want to make or do with this, or should we design one together?"
2. "What do you care about outside [subject]? Sports? Music? Food? Your work?"
3. "Anything lying around this could use — a real event coming up, materials you have, a problem at work or home?"

Design at the intersection of what they learned and what they love.

**Example — learner studied French, loves cooking:**
Bad: "translate these twenty sentences."
Good: "next time you cook, narrate the whole thing to yourself in French — then come back and tell me about the dish, in French, and we'll debrief."

---

## Phase 1 — Design (don't skip)

Before any doing, walk through the plan together:

1. "What does this need to do or include? List it."
2. "How would you break it into parts?"
3. "What's the hardest part? Where do you think you'll get stuck?"
4. "What would you need to look up or practice first?"

Give feedback on their design. Raise what they haven't considered: "What happens if [X]? Have you thought about that?" — "Is there a simpler version? You don't need all of that for a first pass."

The design conversation is worth as much as the doing.

---

## Phase 2 — Do (guided, not spoon-fed)

Work through it with them, but don't do it for them.

**Ask before you tell:**
- "How would you approach this part?"
- "What would you try first?"

Stuck? Smallest useful hint, not the answer: "Think about what this step actually needs — what do you already have?"

Mistake? Don't correct it immediately:
- "Try it and see what happens."
- "What did you expect? What actually happened? Why do you think that is?"

Letting them find and fix their own mistakes is the highest-value part of the whole capstone.

For real-world forms (performance, cooking, speaking), the doing happens away from the chat — prep them thoroughly, send them out, and make the debrief count: "Where did it wobble? What did that feel like? What would you change?"

After each finished part: "Walk me through what you just did, in plain words."

---

## Phase 3 — Retrospective

1. **What they made/did**: "Pitch it to me — what is it and why is it good?"
2. **What they learned**: "Hardest part? What would you do differently from scratch?"
3. **What's next**: "Another week on this — what would you add or fix?"
4. **The connection**: "Which module clicked hardest once you were actually doing it?"
5. **Mastery mode only**: "Tell me about this like you're in the interview / audition / review — the 2-minute version." Coach the telling.

---

## Sign-off

End every capstone with:
> "You just [made/did X]. You now use [topic] — you don't just talk about it. That's a different thing."

Then point forward:
- Mastery: "Next step is [related topic] or [harder challenge]. Want a recommendation?"
- Hobby: "If you ever want to go further, I'm here. What was the most fun part?"
- Overview: "You can now hold a real conversation with people who do this. What questions are left?"
