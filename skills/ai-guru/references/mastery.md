# Mastery Mode — Curriculum Guide

The learner wants deep working competence — to practice this professionally, perform it confidently, or hold their own with people who do. Go deep enough to reason about trade-offs and recover from failure, not just follow instructions.

---

## What mastery means in practice

A mastery learner should be able to:
- Explain core concepts without notes, plainly and precisely
- Handle real problems, not just clean textbook cases
- Evaluate trade-offs ("why X over Y in this situation")
- Talk about failure modes: what goes wrong, why, and what to do about it
- Have done at least one real piece of work they can show or walk through

Don't stop at "they can do it" — push to "they understand why it works."

---

## Curriculum structure

### Module 1 — Foundations and mental model
Goal: an accurate mental model of how this actually works.
- Don't start with techniques or tools. Start with "what problem does this solve and why does it work this way?"
- Ask them to describe the system/skill in their own words before you show the standard picture
- End quiz: explain the core mechanism without using the subject's own jargon

### Module 2 — Core concepts and vocabulary
Goal: precise command of the key building blocks.
- Cover the 5–8 concepts every serious practitioner is expected to know cold
- For each: definition, why it matters, the common misconception
- End quiz: mix recall with "what's wrong with this" questions

### Module 3 — Real-world patterns
Goal: how this is actually done well, outside the classroom.
- Common patterns and idioms of skilled practice
- What good looks like versus what beginners do
- Common mistakes, failure modes, and traps
- End quiz: scenario questions, not recall

### Module 4 — Trade-offs and judgment
Goal: be able to answer "why did you choose X" under questioning.
- When to use this approach versus alternatives
- What it doesn't do well; where it breaks
- Real examples of failure
- End quiz: "walk me through your thinking" questions

### Module 5 — Demonstration prep
Mastery ends with proving it, in whatever form the field respects:
- **Career subjects**: 3–5 real interview questions; give feedback as the interviewer would — "that answer gets you to the next round" or "they'd follow up with X — how do you answer?"
- **Certified trades**: mock certification questions in the real format
- **Arts and performance**: a critique session or audition simulation
- **Languages**: a sustained conversation entirely in the language
- **Professional skills**: a client or stakeholder scenario played end-to-end

Identify their weakest area and drill it. Have them do a "walk me through your work" narrative and coach the telling of it.

---

## Depth signals — push harder when:

- They're right but can't explain why
- They can do it but can't reason about what's happening underneath
- They only describe the happy path — no thought about failure

Say: "That's the right answer. Now the harder version an expert would ask: [follow-up]"

---

## Capstone is mandatory in mastery mode

Say it at the start:
> "For mastery, I'm going to push you to produce something real at the end — something you could show someone in the field. We'll design it together."

The capstone should:
- Use core material from at least 3 modules
- Be open-ended — no step-by-step path to copy
- Be explainable in 2 minutes to a skeptical expert
- Contain at least one "I had to figure something out" moment
