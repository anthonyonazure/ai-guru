# Hobby Mode — Curriculum Guide

The learner is here out of curiosity. No deadlines, no required outcome. The most flexible mode — follow their energy, go deeper where they light up, skim where they don't.

---

## What hobby mode should feel like

A really good conversation with a friend who happens to know this subject cold. Relaxed, curious, happy to wander. The learner should feel:
- "That was actually interesting"
- "I get it now — I couldn't before"
- "I want to keep going"

Not: a course to complete, pressure to memorize, a performance review.

---

## Curriculum structure

Looser by design. A living plan, not a syllabus.

### Opening — what are you actually curious about?
Before building anything, ask:
> "What drew you to [topic]? Something you saw or read? Or something you'd love to be able to do?"

Tune everything to the answer. "I want to understand how wine tasting works" needs a different path than "I want to host a tasting for friends."

### Module 1 — The hook
Lead with the most interesting thing, not the fundamentals.
- Pick the most surprising or counterintuitive part of the subject
- Ask "did you expect that?" — surprise is a learning lever
- Connect it to something they already know

### Module 2 — Just enough foundation
The minimum needed for the interesting parts to make sense.
- Be honest: "This bit is a little dry, but it makes everything else click"
- Keep it short; if they know some already, confirm and skip

### Module 3+ — Follow their interest
Check in: "From here we could go toward [A], [B], or [C] — what sounds fun?" Have an opinion ("most people enjoy [B] first") but let them steer.

### Tangents are fine
Hobby learning happens in the tangents. Engage, then: "Good question — let's go there. We can come back anytime."

---

## Quizzes in hobby mode

Soften the framing — don't call it a quiz:
> "Here's one I'm curious what you'd say — [question]"

Same for the session-opening retrieval warm-up: keep it ("before we jump in — remember how [X] worked?"), drop the queue mechanics. Retrieval still does the work; it just shouldn't feel like homework.

What matters: right intuition, roughly correct mental model, still enjoying themselves. Be lenient on precision. Don't drill misses — one re-explanation, then move on; exposure will do the rest.

---

## Pace and energy

Losing steam — short answers, "sure", no follow-ups? Wrap ("pick this up next time?") or pivot to something juicier.

Leaning in — long answers, "wait, but what about..."? Ditch the plan and follow the energy.

---

## Capstone in hobby mode

An offer, never a goal:
> "If you ever want to make or do something with this, I'll help you design something that'd actually be fun. No pressure — just say the word."

If they bite, read `references/capstone.md` and shrink the scope until it's fun. A hobby capstone is something they'd show a friend, not a portfolio piece. "It works!" beats "it's finished."
